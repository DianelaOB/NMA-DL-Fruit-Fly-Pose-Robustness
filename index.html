Evaluating the Robustness of a U-Net-Based Animal Pose Estimator Using a Fruit Fly Dataset

Computational Neuroscience project developed during the Neuromatch Academy Deep Learning course in July 2026. The project uses the official Neuromatch Academy Animal Pose Estimator to evaluate the robustness of a U-Net-based model.

Project at a Glance

Species & Landmarks

Drosophila melanogaster images annotated with 17 anatomical landmarks.

Objective

Evaluate robustness under appearance changes, geometric transformations, and occlusions.

What Are We Trying to Answer?

Does a U-Net-based animal pose estimator learn robust body-part representations, or does it mainly rely on the consistent orientation, position, and appearance of the training images?

Project Abstract

U-Net is an encoder–decoder convolutional neural network with skip connections. Its dense pixel-level predictions can be adapted for landmark localization via heatmaps. We investigated whether U-Net-based models trained on centered Drosophila melanogaster images containing 17 unique features learn robust body-part representations or rely on consistent training-image orientation, position, and appearance.

We used the Neuromatch Academy Animal Pose Estimator with 4,216 annotated training images and 1,800 held-out test images. Robustness was evaluated under appearance perturbations, including Gaussian noise and brightness/contrast changes; geometric perturbations, including rotations and translations; and random, head-only, and full-body occlusions.

Multiple independently initialized models were assessed using paired baseline comparisons, landmark localization error, Percentage of Correct Keypoints (PCK), landmark-level analyses, and summary statistics. We also explored an adaptive learning-rate scheduler, code refinements, an additional insect dataset, and intermediate feature-map visualization using a simplified U-Net.

Across independent runs, the baseline U-Net accurately localized landmarks in unmodified images. Performance was relatively preserved under mild appearance changes but declined more substantially with geometric transformations and occlusion. Landmark-level analyses indicated that sensitivity varied across anatomical points.

Occlusion of key regions substantially altered predicted landmark locations and heatmap structure, suggesting reliance on both local visual evidence and spatial regularities in the training data.

The Neuromatch U-Net learns useful landmark representations but has limited robustness to transformations that violate training-data structure. High performance may therefore partly reflect consistent fruit fly position, orientation, and visibility. Quantitative perturbation and occlusion analyses offer a framework for evaluating animal pose-estimation generalizability beyond standard test-set accuracy.

Evaluating the Robustness of a U-Net-Based Animal Pose Estimator Using a Fruit Fly Dataset

A computational neuroscience project investigating how reliably a U-Net-based pose estimation model localizes fruit fly anatomical landmarks under appearance changes, geometric transformations, and occlusion.

Project origin

Neuromatch Academy Deep Learning Project

This project was developed during the Neuromatch Academy Deep Learning course in July 2026, Slot 5, as part of the Computational Neuroscience project track.

The work builds upon the official Neuromatch Academy Animal Pose Estimation project, which uses a U-Net-based model to predict anatomical landmarks in fruit fly images.

Our project extends the original implementation by evaluating how robust the model is when the input images differ from the conditions represented in the original training dataset.

Overview

About the Project

Animal pose estimation can support the quantitative study of movement, posture, and behavior in neuroscience and biology. However, strong performance on an original test dataset does not necessarily guarantee reliable predictions under new imaging conditions.

In this project, we investigate the robustness and generalizability of a U-Net-based animal pose estimator trained to localize anatomical landmarks in fruit fly images.

We examine how the model responds to controlled changes in image appearance, geometric structure, and visibility. We also analyze whether individual anatomical landmarks differ in their sensitivity to these perturbations.

Research questions

Project Objectives

1

Evaluate the baseline U-Net pose estimator on unmodified fruit fly images.

2

Measure how model predictions change under controlled image perturbations.

3

Compare the sensitivity of different anatomical landmarks.

4

Investigate the effect of geometric transformations and image-region occlusion.

5

Assess whether model accuracy depends on regularities in fruit fly position, orientation, and visibility.

6

Develop a framework for evaluating robustness beyond standard test-set accuracy.

Experimental approach

Methods

We used the U-Net animal pose estimation framework provided through the Neuromatch Academy Animal Pose Estimation project. The model produces heatmaps representing the predicted locations of fruit fly anatomical landmarks.

Baseline performance was first evaluated using unmodified images. We then examined model behavior under appearance modifications, geometric transformations, and occlusion. Predictions were analyzed across independent runs and at the level of individual anatomical landmarks.

Main findings

Results

Across independent runs, the baseline U-Net localized fruit fly landmarks accurately on unmodified images.

Preliminary perturbation analyses showed that model performance was relatively preserved under mild appearance changes but degraded more strongly under geometric transformations and occlusion.

Landmark-level analyses further suggested that some anatomical points were more sensitive to perturbation than others.

Occlusion experiments indicated that removing key image regions could substantially alter predicted landmark locations and heatmap structure. This suggests that the model depends on both local visual evidence and the spatial regularities present in the training dataset.

Interpretation

Conclusion

Our results suggest that the Neuromatch U-Net animal pose estimator learns useful landmark representations but has limited robustness to input transformations that violate the structure of the training data.

This indicates that high performance on the original dataset may partly reflect reliance on consistent fruit fly position, orientation, and visibility.

Quantitative robustness testing and occlusion-based analyses provide a useful framework for evaluating the generalizability of animal pose estimation models beyond standard test-set accuracy.
